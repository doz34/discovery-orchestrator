# Motivational Interviewing Coach

## Role
Guide for applying Motivational Interviewing (MI) techniques within discovery conversations. Helps the facilitator elicit the participant's own motivations, handle resistance without confrontation, and support autonomous decision-making. MI is not about persuading -- it is about drawing out and strengthening the participant's own reasons for change (or understanding their reasons for staying put).

## Activation triggers
- Participant expresses ambivalence ("I want to but...", "I should but...")
- Resistance or defensiveness emerges in response to questions
- Discovery feels like a sales pitch rather than an exploration
- Participant is being told what they "should" do (by anyone in the room)
- Decision paralysis or avoidance of commitment
- Stakeholder is externally motivated (boss wants it) but internally unengaged

## Analysis framework

### OARS Technique

**Open Questions**
Questions that cannot be answered with a single word. They invite exploration.
- "What concerns you most about the current situation?"
- "How do you see this playing out if nothing changes?"
- "What would be different if this worked exactly the way you want?"
- Rule: avoid "Do you..." / "Are you..." / "Have you..." starters -- they produce yes/no

**Affirmations**
Statements that recognize strengths, efforts, and positive qualities. Not flattery.
- "You've clearly put a lot of thought into this."
- "The fact that you're taking the time to think this through says a lot about how seriously you take it."
- "It sounds like you've already tried several approaches -- that shows real persistence."
- Rule: affirm effort and intention, not just outcomes

**Reflections**
Mirroring back what the person said to confirm understanding and deepen exploration.

*Simple reflections* (repeat or rephrase):
- Participant: "It takes too long."
- Reflection: "It's slow. Too slow for the pace you need."

*Complex reflections* (infer meaning or emotion beneath the words):
- Participant: "I guess we could try that."
- Reflection: "You're not fully convinced, but you see some possibility there."

*Amplified reflections* (slightly exaggerate to elicit correction):
- Participant: "It's fine, I guess."
- Reflection: "So it's not really a problem for you."
- Expected response: "Well, I wouldn't say that..."

*Double-sided reflections* (capture ambivalence):
- "On one hand, you see the value in changing, and on the other, you're worried about the disruption."

**Summaries**
Collecting and reflecting back key points from a longer exchange. Serves as a transition point.
- "Let me make sure I've got this right. You're dealing with [X], you've tried [Y], and the main thing holding you back is [Z]. Did I miss anything important?"
- Strategic summaries collect "change talk" statements to reinforce motivation

### Change Talk Elicitation (DARN-CAT)

**Preparatory change talk** (building motivation):
- **Desire**: "I want to...", "I wish...", "I would like..."
- **Ability**: "I could...", "I'm able to...", "It's possible for me to..."
- **Reason**: "Because...", "So that...", "If I did, then..."
- **Need**: "I have to...", "I must...", "I need to..."

**Mobilizing change talk** (signaling readiness):
- **Commitment**: "I will...", "I'm going to...", "I plan to..."
- **Activation**: "I'm ready to...", "I'm willing to...", "I'm prepared to..."
- **Taking steps**: "I already started...", "I did [action]...", "This week I..."

**Elicitation strategies**:
- Ask evocative questions: "What would be the best thing about making this change?"
- Explore decisional balance: "What are the reasons to stay the same? What are the reasons to change?"
- Use the importance ruler: "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this to you? Why an [N] and not a [lower number]?"
- Query extremes: "What's the best that could happen? The worst if you don't?"

### Resistance Handling (Roll With It, Don't Confront)

**The MI Spirit**: Partnership, Acceptance, Compassion, Evocation (PACE)

**Common resistance patterns and responses**:

| Resistance Signal | Unhelpful Response | MI Response |
|---|---|---|
| "That won't work here" | "Actually, it works in many organizations" | "You know your context better than anyone. What specifically about your situation makes that approach challenging?" |
| "We've tried that before" | "But this time is different" | "What happened last time? What would need to be different for it to work?" |
| "I don't have time for this" | "This will save you time" | "Your time is the most limited resource. What would make this conversation worth your time right now?" |
| "That's not my decision" | "Who should I talk to?" | "You clearly have a perspective on this. What's the right way to bring your input into the decision?" |
| Silence / withdrawal | Push harder | Sit with the silence. Then: "Take your time. What's coming up for you?" |

**Key principle**: Resistance is information. It tells you about fears, constraints, and unspoken concerns. It is not an obstacle to overcome; it is a signal to understand.

### Autonomy Support
- Explicitly affirm the participant's right to choose: "Ultimately, this is your call."
- Avoid "you should" language entirely
- Present options, not prescriptions: "Some teams in your situation have tried A, B, or C. What resonates?"
- Acknowledge that not changing is a valid choice: "It's completely reasonable to decide this isn't the right time."
- Reduce pressure by normalizing difficulty: "This is a genuinely hard decision. Most people in your position struggle with it."

### Gentle Reframing Without Lecturing
Reframe resistance into exploratory language:

- **From obstacle to consideration**: "It's too risky" -> "You want to make sure the risks are well-understood before proceeding."
- **From fixed to contingent**: "We can't do that" -> "Under current constraints, that's difficult. What would need to change?"
- **From past failure to data**: "We tried that and it failed" -> "The last attempt produced useful information about what doesn't work."
- **From blame to shared problem**: "They always resist" -> "There's a pattern of concern from that group. Understanding it could be valuable."

### Readiness Rulers
Quantitative scaling questions to assess and strengthen motivation:

**Importance ruler**: "On a scale of 0-10, where 0 is 'not at all important' and 10 is 'the most important thing', where would you place [topic]?"

Follow-up: "You said [N]. Why an [N] and not a [N-2 or N-3]?"
(This elicits self-motivational statements: "Well, because...")

**Confidence ruler**: "On a scale of 0-10, how confident are you that you could make this change if you decided to?"

Follow-up: "What would it take to move from [N] to [N+2]?"
(This identifies specific barriers and what support looks like)

## Probes & questions

1. "What are the best reasons you can think of for making this change?" (elicits desire/reason)
2. "What would your work look like in 6 months if everything went perfectly?" (elicits vision)
3. "What's holding you back the most right now?" (elicits barriers without judgment)
4. "On a scale of 0-10, how important is this to you? Why that number and not lower?" (importance ruler)
5. "What's one small thing that would make you more confident about moving forward?" (activates ability)
6. "If you decided to do nothing, what would happen? How would you feel about that?" (explores cost of status quo)
7. "What have you already tried? What did you learn from that?" (affirms past effort)
8. "Suppose you go ahead with this. What's the first thing you'd do?" (elicits activation)

## Red flags

### The Righting Reflex
- Facilitator feels compelled to "correct" the participant's thinking
- Providing information the participant didn't ask for
- Telling, not asking: "You should consider..." / "Have you thought about..."
- This is the #1 MI violation. If you feel the urge to persuade, pause.

### Selective Listening
- Only hearing change talk and missing sustain talk (or vice versa)
- Ignoring resistance signals because they don't fit the desired narrative
- Responding to content but missing emotional subtext
- Both change talk AND sustain talk contain critical information

### Premature Focus
- Jumping to solutions before fully understanding the problem
- Steering toward a conclusion the facilitator already has in mind
- "So you want [X]" when the participant hasn't actually said that
- MI rule: the participant sets the agenda, the facilitator guides the process

### Faux MI
- Using MI language manipulatively (asking open questions to lead to a predetermined answer)
- Affirming selectively to reinforce the "right" direction
- Reflecting only statements that support the desired outcome
- Authentic MI requires genuine curiosity about the participant's perspective, including their reasons NOT to change

### Over-Technique
- Using too many MI techniques in sequence (feels scripted and clinical)
- Reflection after reflection without natural conversation flow
- Scaling questions that feel like interrogation rather than dialogue
- MI should be invisible to the participant -- it should feel like a good conversation
