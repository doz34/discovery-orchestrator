# Phase 3: Motivation

## Goal
Uncover the deep personal and organizational motivations beneath the surface request.

## Entry condition
Friction and costs are quantified from Phase 2. The user has confirmed the status quo is expensive enough to warrant change.

## Actions

1. **Ask the "five whys" adapted for motivation.** After the user states what they want, ask why it matters to them personally. Then ask why that matters. Go three to five levels deep. Stop when you hit an emotional or values-level answer, not a functional one.

2. **Distinguish between stated need and deep need.** The stated need is what they ask for ("a dashboard"). The deep need is what they actually want ("to feel in control," "to impress the board," "to stop worrying at 2am"). Record both.

3. **Map competing motivations.** Most projects serve multiple motivations simultaneously. Common patterns:
   - **Growth vs. stability**: "I want to scale, but I don't want to break what works"
   - **Speed vs. quality**: "I need it fast, but it has to be right"
   - **Autonomy vs. support**: "I want to own this, but I need help"
   - **Visibility vs. safety**: "I want to be recognized for this, but I don't want to be blamed if it fails"

4. **Identify whose motivation this is.** Is this the user's own motivation, their boss's, their team's, their client's? Motivations cascade and conflict across stakeholders. Map who wants what and why.

5. **Define success in human terms.** Move beyond deliverable language. Instead of "success is a working app," capture: "success is I stop getting weekend calls about this" or "success is my team stops dreading standup." Ask: "When this is done and working, what does your Tuesday look like?"

6. **Detect values in tension.** Values are non-negotiable principles that shape decisions. Common values in projects: simplicity, comprehensiveness, speed, thoroughness, autonomy, collaboration, transparency, privacy. When two values conflict, the project has a hard trade-off that must be surfaced now.

7. **Validate the motivation map.** Read it back: "I'm hearing that what you really need is [deep need], because [motivation]. The tension you're navigating is [competing motivations]. Success looks like [human description]. Did I get that right?"

## Suggested prompts

- "Why does this matter to you personally — not just professionally?"
- "When this is done and working perfectly, what changes about your day-to-day life?"
- "What would make you feel like this was worth the effort, even if everything else went wrong?"
- "Is there a version of this project succeeding that would still feel like a failure to you? What would that look like?"
- "Who else needs this to work, and what does success look like from their perspective?"

## Exit condition
Deep needs are articulated (not just stated needs), competing motivations are mapped, and success is defined in human terms validated by the user.

## Output
- Stated need vs. deep need mapping
- Competing motivations inventory (with stakeholder attribution)
- Values-in-tension list
- Human-language success definition
- Motivation map validated by user
