# Consulting & Diagnostic Adapter

- client, customer, organization, company, leadership

- business problem, challenge, opportunity, initiative
- diagnostic, assessment, audit, review, analysis
- engagement, proposal, SOW, scope of work, deliverable
- budget, investment, ROI, cost-benefit, business case
- timeline, deadline, milestone, phase, checkpoint
- strategy, roadmap, transformation, change, improvement
- workshop, interview, survey, focus group, discovery session
- maturity, capability, readiness, benchmark, gap analysis

## Phase customization

### Emphasize

- **Stakeholder mapping**: In consulting engagements, who wants what and why is the central question. Map sponsors, champions, resistors, and influencers. Understand their individual success metrics, political stakes, and decision authority. This is not optional; it is the foundation.
- **Problem framing**: Clients often present symptoms, not root causes. "We need a new CRM" is a solution in search of a problem. Discovery must peel back to the underlying business problem and validate that the stated problem is the real problem.
- **Assumption challenging**: Every client carries assumptions about what is wrong and what the solution looks like. Discovery must surface and test these assumptions explicitly. Unchallenged assumptions become unspoken requirements that constrain the engagement.
- **Outcome expectations**: What does success look like for each stakeholder? These expectations often conflict. Discovery must surface and reconcile divergent outcome expectations before the engagement scope is set.

### Skip (rarely useful)

- **Technical architecture**: Unless the engagement is a technical assessment, deep technical discovery is premature. Focus on business outcomes and organizational readiness.
- **Detailed implementation planning**: Consulting discovery frames the problem and defines the engagement scope. Implementation planning comes after the engagement is scoped and sold.

### Modify

- **Constraints**: Split into organizational constraints (culture, politics, change capacity), budget constraints (fixed vs. variable, approval process), and timeline constraints (hard deadlines, competing priorities, board reporting cadence).
- **Risks**: Split into engagement risks (scope creep, stakeholder misalignment, access restrictions) and outcome risks (organizational resistance, market changes, technology shifts during engagement).
- **Success criteria**: Express as business outcomes: revenue impact, cost reduction, process efficiency, customer satisfaction improvement, compliance achievement. Tie every deliverable to a measurable business outcome.

## Output transformation

Generic discovery outputs become consulting-specific artifacts:

- **Problem framing** becomes a problem statement with root cause analysis, symptom-to-cause mapping, and evidence supporting the diagnosis.
- **Stakeholder map** becomes a power/interest matrix with named individuals, their stakes, their influence, and their preferred outcomes.
- **Constraints** becomes an engagement constraints document: budget parameters, timeline windows, access restrictions, political sensitivities, decision-making processes.
- **Risks** becomes an engagement risk register with probability, impact, mitigation strategies, and escalation paths.
- **Success criteria** becomes a set of measurable business outcomes tied to KPIs, with baseline measurements and target improvements.
- **Questions** becomes a diagnostic interview guide with open-ended questions organized by stakeholder group.

## Domain-specific questions

1. Who is the executive sponsor, and what personal stake do they have in this engagement succeeding or failing?
2. What is the stated business problem, and what evidence do you have that this is the root cause rather than a symptom?
3. What has been tried before to solve this problem, what were the results, and why did it not work?
4. What does success look like in specific, measurable terms, and do all key stakeholders agree on that definition?
5. What organizational constraints will shape this engagement: politics, culture, change fatigue, competing initiatives, recent layoffs?
6. What is the budget envelope, how was it determined, and what is the approval process for scope changes?
7. Who will resist this engagement or its recommendations, and what are their concerns?
8. What happens if this engagement produces recommendations that challenge the current strategy or organizational structure?

## Common pitfalls in this domain

1. **Solution-selling disguised as discovery**: The consultant arrives with a predetermined solution and uses discovery to justify it rather than investigate the actual problem. The engagement scope reflects what the consultant wants to sell, not what the client needs. Discovery must remain genuinely open-ended.
2. **Stakeholder tunnel vision**: Only interviewing the sponsor and their direct reports. The people closest to the problem are often several layers down. Discovery that does not reach frontline operators, middle management, and cross-functional partners produces recommendations that are theoretically sound but practically impossible.
3. **Ignoring organizational readiness**: Producing brilliant recommendations that the organization cannot absorb. Discovery must assess change capacity, political will, skill gaps, and competing priorities. A perfect plan that cannot be executed is worse than a good-enough plan that can.
4. **Scope creep through ambiguous problem framing**: The initial problem statement is vague, so every conversation expands the scope. Discovery must continuously test: "Is this in scope or is this a new problem worth a separate engagement?" Document scope boundaries explicitly and revisit them.
5. **Confusing activity with progress**: Scheduling more interviews, holding more workshops, and producing more slides. Discovery should converge, not expand. Every additional conversation should sharpen the problem framing, not open new threads.
