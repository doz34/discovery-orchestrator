## Phase customization

### Emphasize

- **Emotional mapping**: Personal decisions carry emotional weight that cannot be separated from rational analysis. Discovery must surface the emotions: fear, excitement, grief, ambition, guilt, hope. These are not noise; they are data about what matters to the person.
- **Values clarification**: Most personal decisions are values conflicts in disguise. "Should I take the promotion or spend more time with family?" is not a logistics problem; it is a values problem. Discovery must surface the underlying values and their relative priority.
- **Energy assessment**: Will this decision increase or decrease the person's energy, motivation, and sense of agency? Burnout, resentment, and loss of enthusiasm are real costs that rational analysis often ignores.
- **Feasibility of next steps**: Personal decisions fail when the next step is too large or too vague. Discovery must produce concrete, small, time-bound next actions rather than sweeping life plans.

### Skip (rarely useful)

- **Technical constraints**: Unless the decision is about a technical career change, technical discovery is irrelevant.
- **Stakeholder mapping (formal)**: The stakeholders are family, partner, close friends. Formal stakeholder mapping is overkill; a simple "who else is affected by this decision" is sufficient.

### Modify

- **Constraints**: Frame as life constraints: financial runway, family obligations, health considerations, geographic ties, time availability, energy capacity.
- **Risks**: Frame as personal risks: financial risk, relationship risk, identity risk (who am I if I change?), opportunity cost, regret potential.
- **Success criteria**: Express as personal outcomes: alignment with values, energy level, sense of agency, relationship quality, financial stability. Avoid external status metrics unless the person explicitly values them.
- **Boundaries**: Frame as time boundaries (decision deadline), scope boundaries (what is on the table vs. off the table), and commitment boundaries (reversible vs. irreversible decisions).

## Output transformation

Generic discovery outputs become personal-decision-specific artifacts:

- **Problem framing** becomes a values tension map: which values are in conflict, what is the nature of the tension, and what would resolution look like for each value.
- **Constraints** becomes a life constraints assessment: financial, relational, geographic, temporal, and energy constraints with their relative rigidity.
- **Emotional mapping** becomes an emotional landscape: what feelings arise when considering each option, what fears are present, what excites, what feels like relief.
- **Risks** becomes a personal risk assessment: what is the worst that could happen for each path, what is reversible vs. irreversible, what would cause regret.
- **Success criteria** becomes a personal outcomes framework: what does a good decision feel like, what does the person want to be able to say in one year, five years.
- **Questions** becomes a guided self-reflection with prompts designed to bypass rationalization and access genuine preferences.

## Domain-specific questions

1. When you imagine having already made this decision, which outcome makes you feel relief, and which makes you feel dread?
2. What are you afraid of losing if you choose one path over the other? What are you afraid of never having?
3. What does your ideal Tuesday look like in two years? Walk through the whole day.
4. Who in your life is most affected by this decision, and what do they think about it? Have you asked them?
5. If money were not a factor, what would you choose? What does the gap between that answer and your current leaning tell you?
6. What is the smallest step you could take in the next week to test this decision without committing to it?
7. What advice would you give a close friend who was facing this exact same decision?
8. What is the decision deadline, and what happens if you do not decide by then?

## Common pitfalls in this domain

1. **Over-rationalizing personal decisions**: Treating a values conflict as a logistics problem. Generating elaborate pros-and-cons lists, spreadsheets, and decision matrices when the real issue is emotional or identity-related. The person does not need more analysis; they need clarity on what they actually want and fear.
2. **Imposing external values**: The facilitator's values leaking into the discovery process. Privileging ambition, risk-taking, or stability because those are the facilitator's values, not the person's. Discovery must surface the person's own values, not recommend values to adopt.
3. **Ignoring the body**: Personal decisions have somatic signals: tension, exhaustion, energy, restlessness. Discovery that stays purely cognitive misses critical information. The person's physical response to imagining each option is often more honest than their verbal analysis.
4. **Planning too far ahead**: Personal decisions made in five-year plans rarely survive contact with reality. Discovery should focus on the next concrete step rather than the full life roadmap. A clear next step with a review date is more valuable than a ten-year vision.
5. **Confusing exploration with commitment**: Treating the discovery conversation as if the person has already decided to change. Discovery is about understanding the situation, not committing to action. The person should feel more clarity after discovery, not more pressure.
